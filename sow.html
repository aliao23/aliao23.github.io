Defining the problem

In 2017, the UK Financial Conduct Authority found that only a fifth of the 18-24 population "felt as confident as older adults in their ability to choose pensions, investments or mortgages by themselves." Moreover, the great majority of 18-24 year olds "had not had much time to build up savings," and as a result had "little protection against unexpected life events."


Although none of us are very familiar with fintech, we wanted to create an educational tool for a younger age range (14+), and imagined something that would share the simplicity and effectiveness of Duolingo.


We looked at research studies such as L√ºhrmann, Serra-Garcia and Winter (2013), which found that teenagers‚Äô interest is a first step towards increasing their financial knowledge and engagement. Since the literature we read emphasized motivation as a major factor in developing financial literacy, we decided that a gamified approach could be key to encouraging consistency and long-term learning.

Competitive analysis

We looked at 13 similar apps and websites, ranging from Wealthsimple, a well-known Canadian investing platform for individuals that have no prior experience, to RoosterMoney, a British pocket money app. We then simplified the most notable features and ideas into four bullet points, or key takeaways:

  • Incentives through gamification: reward levels, points system, etc., ensure that users are invested in their learning progress

  • Short videos are better primed to match younger users' attention spans and keep them fully engaged

  • Topics are split by complexity level (with optional proficiency or placement testing?) so new users are not overwhelmed by the amount of information

  • Glossaries and definition lists provide a good point of reference so that users don't feel put off by jargon
