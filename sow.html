Defining the problem

In 2017, the UK Financial Conduct Authority found that only a fifth of the 18-24 population "felt as confident as older adults in their ability to choose pensions, investments or mortgages by themselves."


Although none of us were very familiar with fintech, we wanted to create an educational tool for a younger audience (14+), and imagined something that would share the simplicity and effectiveness of Duolingo.


We looked at research studies such as L√ºhrmann, Serra-Garcia and Winter (2013), which found that teenagers‚Äô interest is a first step towards increasing their financial knowledge and engagement.


Since the literature we read emphasized motivation as a major factor in developing financial literacy, we decided that a gamified approach could be key to encouraging consistency and long-term learning.

Competitive analysis

We looked at 13 similar apps and websites, ranging from Wealthsimple, a well-known Canadian investing platform for individuals that have no prior experience, to RoosterMoney, a British pocket money app. We then simplified the most notable features and ideas into four bullet points, or key takeaways:

  • Incentives through gamification: reward levels, points system, etc., ensure that users are invested in their learning progress

  • Short videos are better primed to match younger users' attention spans and keep them fully engaged

  • Topics are split by complexity level (with optional proficiency or placement testing?) so new users are not overwhelmed by the amount of information

  • Glossaries and definition lists provide a good point of reference so that users don't feel put off by jargon


We took a quick break to grab food and join some workshops (Advanced Figma and cross-functional collaboration), and then started constructing personas.

Personas

Time constraints meant that we weren't able to conduct user interviews, so we took insights and quotes from the research studies we read to develop five personas with ages ranging from 15 to 27.

Our persona explorations: click to view each

We tried to include a range of needs and situations, with each "user" being at a different stage of life: 27-year-old Wes, for example, needs to learn how to navigate mortgage payments and support his young family, while 16-year-old Emma wants to start saving for college.


Our personas in Figma

We found that many of our personas had overlapping goals in terms of achieving basic financial literacy to inform their spending or saving decisions, and were intimidated by financial/institutional jargon.

Ideating and wireframing

We really liked the "piggy bank as mascot" concept, and brainstormed a bunch of potential names for our app (sadly, COINK was already taken). We decided to go with with sow, in the context of both a female pig as well as the verb used in the common idiom "reap what you sow," which we felt was especially applicable to what we were trying to accomplish.

Brainstorming product names

We then started thinking about the screens that might be included, and what a user flow might look like in the app.

  • Registration and login

  • Home screen with navigation bar

  • Achievement screen to reward progress with in-app currency (essential for gamification)

  • Store/shop to redeem in-app currency

  • Personal profile, with customisable avatar

  • A course selection screen, and a secondary "lesson" or "topic" selection screen

  • A quiz screen

  • News section with articles sourced from FT, BBC etc.

  • A searchable glossary?

  • An optional diagnostic assessment for placement?

  • A leaderboard to compete with friends?

In the interest of time, we decided to leave out a few that we thought could be added later on, and moved on to our low-fidelity wireframes.
