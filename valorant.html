Idea

How might we adapt a mechanics-intensive PC shooter to a mobile format?

Intro

VALORANT is a 5v5 character-based tactical shooter created by Riot Games. At launch, it broke Twitch’s single-day viewership record with 34 million hours watched and a peak viewership of 1.7 million.


I’ve been talking with James Nguyen, Riot’s former Director of Engineering, who encouraged me to try and develop a design for a mobile adaptation in my own time.


I began to explore flows with two questions in mind:

  • What core elements of the game experience need to be included?

  • What have other adapted mobile games done to accommodate players?

State of play

Unsurprisingly, VALORANT has lots of features and interactions.




A (simplified?) IA diagram of VALORANT's PC features

It’s unlikely that I’ll be able to fully “translate” all the features in the PC version to mobile, so I outlined my top three and prioritised working on the in-game interface (saving the others for future projects).

  • 1. In-game interface

    HUD, scoreboard, and buy screen accessible for players after they enter a game lobby


  • 2. Home menus (for later)

    Launch screen after logging in. Access to the shop, game queue, friends list, missions, and so on


  • 3. Career (for later)

    Match history, rank progress, lobby scoreboard

Interactions and inputs

In Valorant, quick reactions and muscle memory are rewarded.


There are over 22 keys that users are able to interact with ingame. The majority are movement-based (crouch, jump, run), with a heavy skew towards the left side of the keyboard since users are expected to use mice with their right hand.


Mouse inputs are incredibly important in FPSes. Precision in aiming is key, as is aiming down sights and being able to look (and turn) around quickly to react to opponents.

PC HUD breakdown (screenshot from IDDQD)

“Special interactions,” such as planting the spike and interacting with objects on the map, are also core parts of objective-focused gameplay.

Exploring mobile screen interactions

I started with my own reach and grip, and then observed 10 other users. I have particularly short thumbs (thanks, Dad), so I was only able to cover around maybe half to two thirds of the screen area.



Key takeaways
  • 1. No index fingers?

    The majority of casual users felt uncomfortable bringing their index fingers down. It covers a lot of valuable screen space and requires a shift in grip that makes holding the phone less stable.


  • 2. There is a natural arc

    Key interactible elements such as shoot, move, etc. should therefore be in the "natural" arc that thumbs cover in a horizontal grip.

  • 3. Thumb flex

    It’s easier for thumbs to bend inward rather than stretch outward; frequently used buttons should be closer to the bottom corners and edges rather than the top.

With a rough idea of what the norm was for mobile grips, I moved on to player interviews.

Player interviews

Given the wide range of agents available in VALORANT, I wanted to better understand the variety of players’ playstyles and experiences.

The VALORANT players I interviewed could be categorised based on their time spent in game and their prior game experience.


  • The veterans

    Those with 4+ years playing FPS or PC games in general placed heavy emphasis on pixel-perfect precision and immediate network feedback. Only one had ever played mobile shooters (Fortnite and PUBG Mobile), and all were sceptical about whether that sense of high-level skill could transfer to a mobile format.


  • Learners

    More recent to the FPS genre with less experience, valuing the satisfaction of making plays and getting kills. These players spoke about being influenced from Twitch streams, and prioritised their learning curve and agent mastery.


  • Social players

    More casual with their time spent, valuing the social experience, learning collectively and often playing in groups of friends. This group had the most experience with mobile games, but not necessarily mobile shooters.


Market analysis

While interviewing users, one suggested potentially using the volume button to aim down sights or shoot as a way to circumvent visual clutter on screen.


I thought this was a fascinating idea, but wasn’t sure whether it was practically feasible given the constraints of prototyping software, so decided to take a look at how other mobile shooters were solving for visual overload.



Mobile FPS games like Genshin Impact and PUBG Mobile place visual elements such as system status and settings buttons towards the top of the screen, and attack and move buttons near the bottom corners.


Both Fortnite and PUBG Mobile let the user customise their own input style, resulting in some unorthodox ways of interaction like the “claw grip.”


Creating initial prototypes
Initial intended space & feature distribution on mobile

I mocked up a quick prototype to do some field testing. While I knew that the arc thumb range was the most natural, I wasn’t sure about button size or the legibility of visual elements.


User testing insights

Unsurprisingly, there are a lot of tradeoffs that need to be made when designing a game for a mobile screen:


  • 1. Thumb positioning

    Thumbs more naturally rest near the middle of the screen when held horizontally; the buttons for moving and shooting should therefore be higher up.

  • 2. Fitts’ Law

    While bigger, closer targets are easier to hit than smaller, more distanced targets, it’s probably best to deliberately ensure an extent of difficulty to reach given the game context where misclicking can cost you greatly.


  • 3. Everyone’s different

    While the overall grip style was consistent, users had varying phone and thumb sizes. To optimise for edge cases, it’s most likely better to design for larger thumb sizes on smaller screens.
