The prompt

Create a product, service, or experience for a teen or young adult user seeking mental health support & emotional well-being in 2021.

The problem...

The pandemic has substantially affected the mental health and emotional well-being of teens and young adults.


Nearly 4 in 10 report symptoms of moderate to severe depression — a 25% increase from 2018.


We wanted to rethink how people could engage with others in a more meaningful way online, especially to benefit those who want to feel supported.



... And our solution

Our solution was an app called Ketchup that syncs schedules between friends so that they can cook and meal plan together, simulating the atmosphere during mealtimes at college.


Catch up over a meal. 🥘


Cook together with friends (without the awkward scheduling texts). We'll sync your calendar automatically!

Grow your own recipe book. 🛒


Find healthy recipes suited to your schedule and diet. We’ll create a grocery list for you.

Empathy and research

We started by researching questions we thought were fundamental to the project, such as:

  • How do we define support?

  • How does healing happen in a virtual space?

  • What barriers or challenges might there be to accessing mental health support?

We looked at studies from the CDC, articles about toxic self-care, Reddit posts, and international datasets to help structure an interview plan before we began reaching out.


Interview questions

Interview demographics

9 participants, most being teenagers and young adults, as well as a career counselor working remotely at a public high school.


Interview goals

Understanding how users experienced the online transition during the pandemic, their social media habits, and their thoughts about mental health accessibility.


Insights

We found common patterns in their daily habits, as well as shared concerns about pandemic mental health & experiences:

  • 1. Routine

    Our users strongly associated mental health & wellbeing with having set times and tasks.


  • 2. Motivation & feeling unfulfilled

    Not having a "physical" separator between work and relaxation made each more difficult, and the online environment made it much harder to envision goals.


  • 3. Social isolation

    All of our users hoped to quickly return to the familiar social atmosphere on campus. For some, online messaging felt less genuine.


  • 4. Inaccessible mental health resources

    Although everyone thought that therapy and professional help was important, most felt that they were expensive and not worth the effort to try and access.

Narrowing our definition and creating personas

We iterated through 13+ personas with different backgrounds, needs, and insights, and eventually ended up with three:

  • 1. The LOA student

    A college student on a leave of absence worried about the transition to non-student, who needs a community to replace the support and resources she was able to access while in school.


  • 2. The student worker

    A first-generation college student from a low income background, who feels overwhelmed by schoolwork and the commitments he has to his temporary job and family.


  • 3. The high school counselor

    A career counselor at public high school who struggles to engage student interest online. She's worried that she's failing to help those she's responsible for, and wants to connect more effectively.

We would have liked to have spent more time on this stage, but time constraints meant that we needed to move on to ideating.

Ideating and wireframing

We used 7 frames to generate 44 “how might we …” prompts.



Our "how might we" questions

However, while these were helpful for thinking of creative ideas, they tended to address only a small aspect of a persona or users' needs, or not at all.



A "script" database idea that we scrapped for diverging too much from our user needs

After receiving feedback from Hopelab and reflecting back on our interviews, we came up with more user-centric concepts:


  • 1. Meal sharing & planning app

    A meal planning app allowing multiple people to feel like they are cooking and eating together, strengthening connections for the student on a leave of absence without hassle.


  • 2. Mood network lamp/bracelet

    A product that lights up in different colours, encouraging friends to reach out, share, and vent to each other when moods match.


  • 3. “Choose your own adventure” planner

    A “choose your own adventure” planner mixing reflection and planning: seeing the bigger picture and goals while also helping the user think about their present.


Low-fidelity concepts for the "choose your own adventure" future planner

Testing, protoyping, testing, iterating, testing

After conducting cognitive walkthroughs with our ideas, we moved forward with prototyping the meal connection app.


We called our final product Ketchup ("catch up"), syncing schedules to enable friends to cook & meal plan together remotely.



Low-fidelity explorations of Ketchup features

After testing with sticky-note screens, we found pain points in the logistics of scheduling and actually meeting online.


We further explored storyboards, and I decided to design a medium-fidelity prototype with more features.


These included:

  • Preferred “calling” platforms in the onboarding process for users to choose preferred communication methods

  • A grocery list linking to the recipes in the recipe book

  • A "sessions" section for users to easily join existing calls with friends, rather than solely relying on pre-scheduled times



Early user flow diagram

Our onboarding section now had three parts: available cook time, diet, and platforms.


We also explored a flow to find recipes based on ingredients that users might already have in their fridge.



After testing with users, I added "stories" or "statuses" accessible from the home page, showing recently active friends and their preferences.


We also created a scheduling page linked to Google Calendar/iCal to suggest potential meeting times, with a "swipe to RSVP" interaction.

Reflecting

It's important to have a strong attention to detail, especially when synthesizing personas to inform development.


Addressing and tackling mental health doesn’t necessarily have to be a tangible intervention: Ketchup showed us that subtle, indirect solutions work just as well.


Circling back to our original interviews and personas helped us better understand why we were doing this work in the first place.


Developing and evolving any good idea takes time. Being the sole product designer and having to create a complete set of screens in 2 days is also pretty hard!
