The prompt

In January 2021, President Biden signed Executive Order 13895, which states:


  • "Affirmatively advancing equity, civil rights, racial justice, and equal opportunity is the responsibility of the whole of our Government ... Agencies must recognize and work to redress inequities in their policies and programs that serve as barriers to equal opportunity."


Use design to envision future experiences of an anti-racist and inclusive National Parks Service.

The problem

The NPS has historically had large disparities in visitorship between white people and people of colour.


Data collected by the NPS Visitor Services Project from 76 studies show that people of colour are a small fraction of visitors:

  • Those from Hispanic and Asian American backgrounds each comprised less than 5% of visitors to surveyed sites.

  • Less than 2% of visitors were Black.


We wanted to develop a solution that would provide a more accessible and appealing National Parks experience to those in the <5%, without making them feel uncomfortable or out of place.

Empathy

We met with 10 different users and used an interview frame with questions such as:

  • Where and how do you usually spend your free time?

  • What was your initial motivation in visiting national parks? Has that changed over time?

  • Has COVID-19 impacted how you think of & interact with public spaces? How?


Notes from a preliminary interview

Of our 10 interviews, two stood out the most:

  • 1. Allison

    A middle school teacher at a low-income school who had pioneered an overnight trip to Donner Pass for 80 students.


  • 2. Antonia

    An immigrant mother who wants her children to experience nature, but struggled to visit National Parks because of difficulties with planning, timing, and feeling unwelcome.

We created 2x2 grids and empathy maps to further explore their experiences.


Key takeaways
  • 1. One size doesn't fit all

    Activities haven't been designed with Allison's students in mind, and the traditionally white structures of the NPs make them feel uninvested and uninterested in the environment.


  • 2. The experience starts before you go

    Antonia feels like visiting NPs isn't an option: she's struggled with trip planning due to the language barrier, which leads to her feeling unwelcome and excluded.

We also generated observations for both online and in-person locations, including websites, live streams, a community garden, and Richmond Park.

Creating personas

We refined insights from our interviews with Allison and Antonia into five personas with different needs. Of these, we chose to explore:


  • 1. The first-timer

    An 8th-grade student of colour from a low-income family experiencing a NP for the first time on an overnight school trip, who needs a reason to be invested in a place that has traditionally not been welcoming.

  • 2. The frustrated mother

    An immigrant mother who wants to immerse her children in the environment, but is overwhelmed by the planning process and is worried about exclusivity.


These inspired 13 “how might we” questions that delved into specific aspects of their needs, including:

  • HMW find common ground between students of colour and white National Park employees?


  • HMW instill a lasting sense of excitement for students of colour experiencing the outdoors for the first time?


  • HMW reduce the pain points an ESL mother experiences before stepping foot in a National Park?


  • HMW bring the park experience to her kids without significantly adding to her schedule?

We eventually came up with 150+ potential solutions.





Our Mural ideation boards
Our solutions: protoyping, testing, and iterating

We chose five different ideas to prototype, using LEGOs, post-it notes, and pez dispensers:


1. The Plant an early seed initiative


The NPS sends parents a “mystery” package for their children. Package contents include:

  • Seeds from plants found across American National Parks

  • A multilingual informational booklet with detailed instructions for planting and caring for the seeds

  • Educational activities to complete in the meantime

  • A brief history of the indigenous land they came from


2. A scavenger hunt app/photo activity

In the style of Pokémon Go, using gamification to encourage and engage children who might be uncomfortable in and/or less used to a rural environment.





3. Park Noise

An interactive product given to first-time visitors, allowing them to hear ranger conversations and park soundscapes at home.


During downtime, the product connects to a NP radio station where people can continue to listen and learn.


4. Monthly bingo

A monthly bingo-style checklist for NP-inspired activities that users could complete in their local community, bringing NP values to them by encouraging them to get outside/explore their local environment.




5. Bring your parents

Recorded videos from parents explaining park features and history to remotely introduce parks to their children.


Young students have a familiar face in a potentially intimidating environment, and something to talk about together when they get back.



After conducting cognitive walkthroughs and getting feedback from the NPS, we realized that it would be difficult to coordinate the first with families which didn't have access to tools.


We then iterated our prototype, proposing a scheme to partner the NPS with local communities and schools to make it more accessible.


Reflecting

This was our first time working on an experience-based project, rather than creating a tangible product or app.


Despite being in multiple time zones, we enjoyed working on the project as a team and seeing it come to life.



Final thoughts and takeaways

We were severely constrained by time (we only had three weeks), and would’ve liked to target the anti-racist part of the prompt in a more head-on way.


If we had more time, I think we could've conducted more interviews based on specific personas and further developed our empathy explorations.
