Created to fulfil the Dunbar Fellowship for the Gould Center for Humanistic Studies

Crafting a Crisis Narrative explores how propaganda has been created in times of crisis to appeal to public consciousness and promulgate particular narratives using visual design and evocative language.

Interview features from Professor Gennifer Weisenfeld and Professor Ryan Engley.


Pictured: Suez propaganda leaflet taken from Egyptian soldier, 1956.

Project background & proposal

During the summer, I was selected as a Dunbar Fellow by the Gould Center for Humanistic Studies to develop an individual project designed to showcase the power of humanities during times of crisis.


Crafting a Crisis Narrative examines how crises — events involving significant social, political or societal upheaval such as pandemics, outbreaks of war, mass protests and revolutions — are depicted in literature, news, film, and other media in order to appeal to public consciousness and promulgate particular narratives, and also aims to identify and explain prominent visual patterns.


The humanities have always served as effective tools of persuasion and discussion, but are often overlooked as impractical and overly idealistic in times of crisis. Propaganda serves as a conspicuous contradiction to this notion of irrelevancy, as well as providing a stark reminder of how powerful the humanities can be in shaping public thought and opinion during crises such as pandemics.


The growth of graphic design as an artistic field in the 20th century has led to the development of a stereotypical view of propaganda, where conventional understanding of propaganda revolves around the distinctive imagery of posters produced by totalitarian states without recognition of its inherently adaptive nature. The creation of the internet and the subsequent shift in how media is consumed has also engendered the development of newer, subtler methods of influencing audiences which are greatly distanced from anything reminiscent of blatant Cold War agitprop.

Logo design and animation

Created in Adobe After Effects. I wanted to design something reminiscent of Bill Nye’s TV show and the BBC’s QI (both of which I highly recommend) for an inquisitive and slightly cheeky feel to the project.

Case studies

I examined five cases of historical moments of crisis: the 1666 Great Fire of London which was preceded by a severe plague, the 1956 Suez Crisis which saw Britain fall from its status as a great power, the 2014-2016 West Africa Ebola outbreak, the ongoing HIV/AIDS crisis, and the 1918 influenza pandemic that was mistakenly labeled as Spanish.
